# The Paperboy Story

*A pitch for selling Paperboy — lead with the story, not the features.*

## The frame

A product story needs three things:

1. **A protagonist** — not "the County," but one named (or nameable) person: the safety officer, the new hire, the admin chasing signatures.
2. **A villain** — not "inefficiency," but the concrete absurdity: a parking form that takes three weeks and two interoffice envelopes, an OSHA log living in a binder nobody can find during an audit.
3. **A turn** — the moment the same task becomes trivially easy, with a number attached.

## The pitch

> Every form in this building used to be a journey. A probation transfer request started as paper on someone's desk, rode interoffice mail to a supervisor who was out that week, came back for a missing signature, and arrived — sometimes — a month later. Nobody could say where it was at any given moment. The form wasn't the work; *finding the form* was the work.
>
> We called the fix **Paperboy**, because that's the job it took over: carrying the paper. An employee fills out the form once, from their desk. It knows who their supervisor is, who needs to approve it, and what order they sign in. Approvers see it in their inbox the same day. Anyone involved can see exactly where it stands — no calls, no envelopes, no "I never got it." When the auditor asks for the OSHA 300A, it's one click, not a binder hunt.
>
> Parking requests, ID badges, leave of absence, safety reports, workplace violence reporting, P-card requests — two dozen forms so far, every one of them tracked, signed, and findable. The paper still exists if you want it: Paperboy will print you a clean PDF. It just doesn't make a person carry it anymore.

## Tuning it per audience

- **Executives / budget owners:** lead with risk and numbers — audit readiness (OSHA logs, workplace violence reporting), cycle time ("3 weeks → same day"), and that it runs on existing County infrastructure.
- **Department heads:** lead with visibility — "you can see every pending request in your unit right now," the authorization console, task reassignment when someone's out.
- **End users:** lead with the inbox — one place, pre-filled with who you are and who you report to.

## Make it true

If you can get **one real anecdote** — an actual form that got lost, an actual audit scramble — swap it in for the generic version above. A true specific story beats a polished invented one every time. Real numbers help too: submission counts, approval turnaround before vs. after.
